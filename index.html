Frequently Asked Questions

How accurate are the calculator projections?
Our calculations use standard mathematical formulas for compound interest. However, real-world returns fluctuate significantly. These projections assume a steady, constant return rate and are strictly for educational and planning purposes, not guaranteed financial advice.

What return rate should I enter?
Historically, the U.S. stock market has returned an average of 7-10% annually before inflation. Many conservative planners use 6% or 7% to account for inflation (often called the "real return rate"), ensuring your future target maintains today's purchasing power.

Does this account for taxes?
No. The calculator computes raw, pre-tax compound growth. Depending on whether you use tax-advantaged accounts (like a Roth IRA or 401k) or standard brokerage accounts, your actual take-home amounts upon withdrawal will vary.

Do you save my financial data?
Absolutely not. FinTrack runs entirely in your local browser. We do not transmit, save, or store any of the personal financial inputs you enter into the calculators.

Building Wealth for Early Retirement

Retiring early (often referred to as the FIRE movement — Financial Independence, Retire Early) requires a shift in how you view savings. Rather than just setting money aside, it requires consistent, long-term investing into assets that grow over time.

The math behind early retirement relies heavily on compound growth. When your money is invested, it generates returns. Over the years, those returns begin to generate their own returns, creating a snowball effect. This is why investing early and consistently is far more powerful than trying to perfectly time the market.

Realistic Scenario

Imagine you want to reach a $1,500,000 portfolio to sustain your early retirement. If you start with a base of $50,000 and consistently invest $1,500 every single month, assuming a conservative 7% annual return, it would take you roughly 23.5 years to reach your goal. What's incredible is that over $1 million of that final balance will be generated purely from interest, not your own pocket.

The Journey to One Million

Becoming a millionaire often doesn't happen overnight. It is typically the result of decades of disciplined financial behavior, maintaining a high savings rate, and letting the stock market do the heavy lifting over time.

Consistency matters significantly more than timing the market. By automating your monthly investments, you ensure that you buy into the market during both highs and lows, building a resilient portfolio that accelerates as your balance grows larger.

The Millionaire Path

If you start with just $10,000 and invest $800 each month at a 7% average return, you will cross the $1,000,000 mark in about 29 years. During that time, you only contributed around $288,000 out of pocket—meaning over $700,000 of your wealth was generated entirely by compound interest.

Why the First $100k is the Hardest

When you start from zero, almost all the growth in your portfolio comes from your own heavy lifting. The interest earned on small amounts is negligible, making the climb feel incredibly slow.

However, once you build up a base of $100,000, compound interest finally starts working alongside you. A 7% return on $100k generates $7,000 in a year—often more than what people can comfortably save manually. Getting past this initial barrier is essential for long-term wealth.

The Snowball Effect

Starting with $5,000 and investing $500 monthly at 7% return, it takes about 10.5 years to hit $100k. But watch what happens next: getting from $100k to your second $100k (hitting $200k) with the exact same inputs takes only 6 more years. That is the power of compounding in action.
